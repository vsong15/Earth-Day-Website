Causes

OCEAN LITTER

Litter in the oceans of the world comes from a variety of sources, including containers falling off ships during storms, trash washing off city streets into rivers leading into the sea, and waste from landfills blowing into streams or directly into the sea. This debris may slowly degrade once in the ocean and persist for years, traveling the currents, accumulating in large patches and washing up on beaches.

PESTICIDES AND FERTILIZERS

Over the last 50 years, the usage of pesticides and fertilizers on farms has risen 26-fold, driving growth over crop production globally. But the environmental implications were severe. Indiscriminate use of pesticides and fertilizers may pollute surrounding soil and water, and as it rains, contaminants will flow onto local lakes, rivers, and wetlands. Pesticides, including beneficial insects, soil bacteria and fish, may destroy non-target species. Fertilizers are not specifically harmful but their existence in freshwater and marine environments can change the nutrient balance. This modification may cause algae to develop explosively due to excess nutrients. As a consequence, the water becomes drained from dissolved oxygen which can destroy fish and other marine organisms

AIR POLLUTION

Air pollution brings to mind images of smokestacks billowing black particles into the atmosphere, but in other respects this emission exists. The combustion of fossil fuels emits large quantities of carbon dioxide into the environment, creating climate change, through both coal plants and cars. Chemical facilities also release particulate matter such as ozone of arsenic, monoxide of carbon and other noxious gases. The indoor areas may become tainted by smoking and cooking pollution. When emitted into the environment, some of those chemicals lead to smog and acid rain. Short term exposure to air pollution can irritate the eyes, nose and throat and cause upper respiratory infections, headaches, nausea and allergic reactions. Long-term exposures may contribute to chronic respiratory disease, pulmonary failure and lung cancer. Long-term emissions may also contribute to major shifts in temperature and can have far-reaching adverse effects on food, water and habitats.

NOISE AND LIGHT POLLUTION

Artificial light and noise frequently drown out natural scenery. The noises of oil and gas explorations in the Arctic are so noisy that belugas, bowhead whales and other marine life have had difficulty grazing and breeding. Light exposure interferes with circadian cycles for both humans and animals, and may also lead to cancer growth. Sea turtles may also be harmed by light emissions. Adult and hatchling sea turtles are attracted around the shore towards lights, believing that they are going towards the sky. Thus coastal projects are advised to shut off their lights or cover them at night

WATER POLLUTION

Clean freshwater is an important component for a stable human life, but 1.1 billion people do not have exposure to water and 2.4 billion people do not have sufficient sanitation. Water is contaminated by the disposal or washing of hazardous chemicals into lakes and rivers and the release of untreated and chemical waste. Such toxins come in multiple forms ‚Äî organic, inorganic, even radioactive ‚Äî which may render it unpleasant for people, livestock and other species alike, if not impossible.

HARMFUL ALGAL BLOOMS AND DEAD ZONES

Human activities, particularly agriculture, have resulted in large environmental increases in the levels of nitrogen and phosphorus. In water, this excess nutrient accumulation, a mechanism called eutrophication, may feed excessive phytoplankton and algae production, which can also have catastrophic implications. Harmful algal blooms ‚Äî blooms of organisms containing toxic toxins and also known as "red tides" or "gray tides" because of their presence in the water ‚Äî can consume fish, aquatic animals and seabirds and damage humans. ACID RAIN

Mild acidic compounds are formed when water in the atmosphere mixes with certain chemicals ‚Äî particularly sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides emitted during burning of fossil fuels. This acid rain will leach radioactive aluminum from the surface and can harm fish in lakes and streams at low rates or destroy them outright at higher concentrations. Acid rain often weakens forest trees and leads to air contamination which can affect human beings.

OCEAN GARBAGE PATCHES

Plastics and other marine debris that can float, traveling the currents, can remain in the oceans for years. Much of this waste is collecting in ocean gyre centres, producing fantastic garbage fields. The word "garbage patch" alludes to floating waste islands, but none of the litter can be seen on the water. Rather, trash patches are places where the amounts of flotsam and jetsam are especially high, often tiny bits of plastic. This litter will spread ocean-wide harmful substances, catch and damage corals, and hurt animals if they eat bits of plastic or get caught in the garbage.

These contaminants grow at greater and greater amounts at livestock throughout the food chain, a mechanism called biomagnification. High predators ‚Äî including fish, insects, and mammals ‚Äî can have far higher rates of such contaminants in their bodies, rendering them more prone to undergo such poisons 'illnesses, birth defects, genetic abnormalities, among other adverse consequences.
